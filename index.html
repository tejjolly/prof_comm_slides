Numerical diffusion

Even a realistic-looking fluid simulation can be secretly wrong.

  • Sharp features can look too “blurred” on the computer.
  • The blur comes from the numerics, not the fluid physics.

I’ll show how this extra smearing appears in CFD and why it matters for engineering decisions based on simulation results.

How CFD stores fluids

CFD turns a continuous fluid into a grid of tiny boxes.

  • The domain is split into many small cells, like pixels.
  • Each cell holds an average velocity, pressure, and concentration.

The solver updates those averages in time, so the computer sees the flow as a collection of boxes instead of a perfectly smooth field.

Where smearing comes from

Some update methods act like a built-in blur filter.

  • Sharp fronts are spread across neighboring cells at each step.
  • Simple, stable schemes can smooth things more than the physics would.

Over time, the simulated front becomes broader and weaker, even if the real fluid would keep a much sharper edge.

Why it matters

Extra numerical blur can hide peaks and hot spots.

  • Smeared peaks can make a plume or hotspot look safer than it is.
  • Thresholds can appear in the wrong place or at the wrong time.

When we base safety margins or treatment choices on peak values and thresholds, numerical diffusion can quietly shift those answers.

Takeaway

Good CFD means managing numerical artifacts, not just running the solver.

  • We tune schemes and grids to keep important features realistic.
  • We check simulations against data to separate physics from artifacts.

The goal is simulations we can trust when real designs and real patients depend on the numbers coming out of the code.
