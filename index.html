Numerical diffusion

Even a realistic-looking fluid simulation can be secretly wrong.

  • Simulations can blur sharp features that stay sharp in real life.
  • This extra “smearing” comes from the numerics, not the physics.

I’ll show how this “numerical diffusion” appears when we solve fluid equations on a computer, and why it matters for the decisions engineers make from CFD results.

How CFD stores fluids

CFD turns a continuous fluid into a grid of tiny boxes of averaged values.

  • The domain is chopped into many small cells, like pixels or Lego bricks.
  • Each cell carries averaged quantities: velocity, pressure, dye level.
  • At each time step, the solver updates those averages using the equations of motion.

This grid-based view is powerful, but it also means the computer only “sees” the fluid in chunks, not with infinite detail.

Where smearing comes from

Some update methods act like a built-in blur filter on sharp features.

  • When a sharp front moves across the grid, we must share it between neighboring cells.
  • Simple, stable schemes do this by averaging more than the physics would.
  • The solution gets smoother every time step, even if the real fluid wouldn’t.

In practice, this means that a sharp dye front or shock wave can look broader and weaker in the simulation than it really is.

Why it matters

Extra numerical blur can mislead engineers about peaks and safety margins.

  • Lower, wider peaks can make a pollutant plume look safer than it is.
  • Artificial mixing can hide sharp temperature or stress hot spots.
  • Time-dependent signals can look washed out or delayed.

Design decisions are often based on “what is the maximum value?” or “where does this threshold get crossed?” Numerical diffusion can change the answers to those questions.

Takeaway

Good CFD means controlling numerical artifacts, not just running the code.

  • We choose schemes and grids that keep important features as sharp as the physics demands.
  • We balance stability against accuracy—too diffusive or too “wiggly” are both problems.
  • We validate against experiments to tell real physics from numerical side-effects.

The goal is not just attractive flow pictures, but simulations we can trust when real people and real designs depend on the results.
